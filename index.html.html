About Me

I grew up in Crown Heights, Brooklyn — a neighborhood with deep cultural roots and real stakes. Watching my mother navigate hardship with discipline and dignity gave me a framework for everything that followed. Finance was never just a career choice. It was a conclusion.

At 10 years old, I read Rich Dad Poor Dad cover to cover and understood for the first time that money was a system — and systems could be learned. By 14, I had started a financial literacy mentoring practice in my community, coaching neighbors on budgeting and credit-building before I had a bank account of my own. That gap between what I understood and what the people around me had access to is what still drives me.

Today I am a Finance & Accounting student at the Eller College of Management at the University of Arizona, carrying a 3.8 GPA and a Mathematics Minor, serving as VP of Finance for the Quantitative Finance Club, and building the technical foundation — DCF modeling, valuation, FP&A, Python, Bloomberg — to compete at the highest level of the industry.

Philosophy & Drive

I don’t separate who I am from how I work. The same discipline that puts me in the gym before sunrise is what keeps me rebuilding a model until the numbers are right. The same curiosity that takes me to a mosque in Istanbul or a temple in Bangkok is what makes me a better analyst — because understanding how people think, believe, and organize their lives is fundamental to understanding markets.

I read Robert Greene the way others read news — systematically, annotating, applying. The 48 Laws of Power, Mastery, The Laws of Human Nature. Not to manipulate, but because self-awareness and strategic thinking are competencies, and competencies can be trained.

Formula 1 taught me that marginal gains compound. A 0.1 second improvement across every corner adds up to a championship. That logic applies to everything — modeling, fitness, preparation, networking. Attention to detail at every margin is not perfectionism. It is competitive advantage.

Money is a necessity. But once you acquire enough of it, the only things you cannot buy are time and health. That’s why the long-term goal has always been generational wealth — not for the number, but for what it represents: optionality, freedom, and the ability to change the trajectory of the people around you.
